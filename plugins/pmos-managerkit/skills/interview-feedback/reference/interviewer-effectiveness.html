Structured questioning weight 16

Asks consistent, pre-planned, role-relevant questions across every candidate so answers are comparable on one rubric.

1234
✓ Green
  • Worked from the round's vetted question set; same core questions asked of comparable candidates.
  • Tied each question to a defined competency on the scorecard.
✕ Red
  • Improvised an ad-hoc line of questioning unique to this candidate.
  • Skipped planned questions, leaving dimensions unassessable.
Probing depth weight 16

Uses follow-ups that get past rehearsed or surface answers to the candidate's actual reasoning, trade-offs, and contribution.

1234
✓ Green
  • Asked "what exactly did you do" / "why that and not the alternative" to separate ownership from team work.
  • Followed a vague claim with a concrete, specific drill-down.
✕ Red
  • Accepted rehearsed headlines without testing the reasoning beneath them.
  • Moved on after the first answer even when it was thin or evasive.
Leading-question avoidance weight 10

Does not telegraph the desired answer; keeps questions open so the candidate, not the interviewer, supplies the substance.

1234
✓ Green
  • Posed neutral, open questions and let silence pull out the answer.
  • Withheld the "right" framing even when the candidate stalled.
✕ Red
  • Baked the expected answer into the question ("you'd of course choose X, right?").
  • Coached a struggling candidate toward the model answer, contaminating the signal.
Talk-time balance weight 10

Candidate speaks substantially more than the interviewer (commonly cited as ~80/20 listening, or at least ~60/40 in the candidate's favor).

1234
✓ Green
  • Candidate held the floor for the large majority of the session.
  • Interviewer's airtime was mostly questions and brief clarifications.
✕ Red
  • Interviewer dominated — long monologues, war stories, or selling the company mid-evaluation.
  • Interrupted or finished the candidate's sentences, suppressing evidence.
Area coverage weight 12

Covers the round's intended competencies in the time available so the loop gets the signal this round was assigned to produce.

1234
✓ Green
  • Touched every competency the round owns; managed time so none were rushed at the end.
  • Avoided redundant overlap with adjacent rounds.
✕ Red
  • Ran out of time with assigned areas unassessed.
  • Over-indexed on one favorite topic and left a competency blank.
Bias mitigation weight 16

Evaluates on observed evidence and resists halo, affinity, and recency bias rather than rating on impression or similarity.

1234
✓ Green
  • Judged behavior against anchors, not "I just liked them" or a single strong first impression.
  • Weighted the whole session, not only the last or most vivid moment.
✕ Red
  • Halo/horn: one trait (school, charisma, a stumble) colored every dimension.
  • Affinity: rated a "like me" candidate up, or a dissimilar one down, on shared background.
Note quality weight 10

Captures specific, quotable evidence — what the candidate actually said and did — rather than adjectives and impressions.

1234
✓ Green
  • Recorded near-verbatim quotes and concrete behaviors tied to each dimension.
  • Notes let a reader reconstruct the evidence without having been in the room.
✕ Red
  • Wrote only impressions ("smart", "weak", "good vibes") with nothing to ground them.
  • Sparse or no notes; relied on memory at debrief.
Calibration weight 10

Ratings are well-anchored to the standardized scale and defensible — the same answer would earn the same score from a trained peer.

1234
✓ Green
  • Scores map cleanly to the rubric's anchor definitions; evidence justifies the number.
  • Stood by a rating with specifics at debrief, and updated when shown contrary evidence.
✕ Red
  • Chronically lenient or harsh, or clusters everyone at "3" to avoid committing.
  • Score and written evidence point in different directions.
