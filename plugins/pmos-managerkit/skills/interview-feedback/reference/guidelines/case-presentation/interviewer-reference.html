Case presentation — interviewer reference

case-presentation round · what to probe, what good and weak answers look like

Purpose of this round. The candidate presents a prepared case — a product decision, a strategy, or a take-home — to a panel and then defends it in live Q&A. This round tests communication and composure under cross-functional scrutiny: can they tell a clear story to a senior, mixed-function audience, lead with the recommendation, and hold their reasoning together when several people push on it at once? It is a proxy for a real PM moment — walking a leadership review or steering committee through a call and absorbing the challenge without losing the thread.
⚠ This is a PANEL round. Multiple interviewers are in the room at once (ties into the skill's interviewer-model panel role). Per-interviewer talk-time and coverage are shared responsibilities: agree before the session who opens, who owns which probe areas, and who watches the clock so the candidate gets a fair, non-overlapping Q&A and every dimension below is actually exercised. Do not let one voice dominate or leave a dimension un-probed because each assumed another would cover it. Each panelist scores this card independently — fill it from your own observation, then calibrate together afterward; do not converge on a number live in the room.

Presentation structure

Whether the talk has a spine. A strong case moves problem → insight → recommendation so the panel can follow without reconstructing the logic themselves. Watch how they open, how each section earns its place, and whether the slides serve the argument or just hold data.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Opens by framing the problem and where it's headed, then delivers a clean arc.
  • Each section sets up the next; the recommendation feels earned, not bolted on.
✕ Watch for
  • A pile of slides with no through-line; the panel assembles the story.
  • Jumps to a conclusion the build never supported.

Suggested probes

  • "In one sentence, what's the story you're telling us here?"
  • "Walk us from the problem to your recommendation — what's the connective tissue?"
  • "If you had half the slides, which would you cut and why?"

Executive communication

Altitude and answer-first instinct. A senior panel wants the recommendation up front, with the reasoning available on demand — not a build-up that makes them wait. Watch whether they pitch to the room's altitude and can flex up or down when a panelist signals they want more or less.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Leads with the answer, then supports it; respects the panel's time.
  • Reads the room and adjusts depth when a panelist wants more or less detail.
✕ Watch for
  • Lives in the weeds; the panel has to ask "so what's your recommendation?"
  • Same altitude regardless of who's asking or what they signal.

Suggested probes

  • "Give me the 30-second version a busy exec would want."
  • "If this were a one-line update to the CEO, what would it say?"
  • "You're deep in the analysis — pull us back up: what's the decision you're asking for?"

Handling pushback & Q&A

How they behave when the room disagrees. The point of a panel is live challenge — watch whether they engage the substance of an objection, change their mind for a good reason without folding for a bad one, and stay composed when challenges come from several directions at once.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Engages the actual objection; concedes a fair point and defends a sound one with reason.
  • Stays composed and curious when several panelists push at once.
✕ Watch for
  • Gets defensive, talks over the challenge, or restates the original claim louder.
  • Folds instantly on any pushback regardless of whether it's right.

Suggested probes

  • "I disagree with your core assumption — convince me, or tell me what would change your mind."
  • "Engineering will say this is twice the scope you think. How does that move your recommendation?"
  • "Two of us just gave you conflicting feedback. How do you reconcile it?"

Depth under scrutiny

Whether the substance is real. Panels drill below the slide — watch whether the reasoning, numbers, and assumptions hold when probed a layer deeper, and whether they actually did the work behind the headline or are running on a thin veneer.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Reasoning holds up when probed; can show the working behind a claim.
  • Knows the soft spots in their own analysis and names them before you do.
✕ Watch for
  • Crumbles one question past the slide; the depth was an illusion.
  • Hand-waves numbers or assumptions they clearly never pressure-tested.

Suggested probes

  • "Where does this number come from, and what would break it?"
  • "What's the weakest assumption in this case, and why did you accept it?"
  • "Show me the step behind that conclusion — why does it follow?"

Message prioritization

Whether they know what matters most. With limited panel attention, a strong candidate makes the single most important point unmistakable and lets the rest recede. Watch whether everything is weighted equally (so nothing lands) or there's a clear hierarchy of what to remember.

✓ Strong looks like
  • One headline takeaway is unmistakable; supporting detail is clearly secondary.
  • Cuts or de-emphasizes the merely-interesting to protect the essential.
✕ Watch for
  • Everything weighted equally; the panel can't tell what the point was.
  • Buries the key insight under detail no one needed.

Suggested probes

  • "If the panel remembers exactly one thing from this, what should it be?"
  • "What did you deliberately leave out, and why didn't it make the cut?"
  • "Rank your three points — which one carries the decision?"

Calibration

  • Score the presenter, not the polish. A confident delivery can mask thin substance, and a nervous one can hide real depth — weight depth-under-scrutiny and handling-pushback on what survives probing, not on stage presence alone.
  • Separate the case from the candidate's prep time. If everyone got the same prompt and window, judge the thinking, not how glossy the deck is.
  • Anchor to the round's bar. A "4" is a presentation you'd let a candidate give to your own leadership unaccompanied — not merely "above average for the loop."
  • Panel calibration is post-hoc. Each panelist scores independently from their own notes first; only then compare. Treat divergence as signal worth discussing, not an error to average away in the room.

Common mistakes (interviewer & candidate)

  • Candidate presents analysis instead of a recommendation. A tour of the data with no decision is a red flag for executive-communication and message-prioritization — probe for the actual call before scoring.
  • Candidate doesn't budget time for Q&A. Burning the full slot on the deck leaves no room for the part this round most measures. Note it, and make sure the panel reserves time so handling-pushback can be observed at all.
  • Panel lets one interviewer dominate. If one voice runs the Q&A, coverage of the other dimensions silently drops — pre-assign probe areas (see the panel note above).
  • Interviewer rewards charisma over substance. Smooth delivery is not depth; always probe one layer past the slide before trusting a high score.
  • Interviewer converges on a score live. Nodding along to the loudest panelist contaminates independent judgment — hold your number until the debrief.
