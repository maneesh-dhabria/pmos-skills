Product sense — interviewer reference

product-sense round · what to probe, what good and weak answers look like

Purpose of this round. This round tests product thinking: whether the candidate can empathize with real users, structure an ambiguous problem before reaching for solutions, generate creative and differentiated ideas, prioritize with explicit tradeoffs, and define what success means with the right metrics. Score the reasoning path, not whether you personally like the final idea.

User empathy

Does the candidate identify and segment the real users and name pains grounded in evidence rather than projecting their own preferences onto the whole market?

✓ Strong looks like
  • Names specific user segments and the distinct job each is trying to get done
  • Cites evidence (interviews, data, observed behavior) for the pains they assert
✕ Watch for
  • Treats "everyone" as one undifferentiated user
  • Names themselves as the user and assumes their preference generalizes

Suggested probes

  • Who exactly is this for, and who is it explicitly not for?
  • What pain does that segment feel today, and how do you know it's real?
  • Where do those users differ from you, and how does that change the design?

Good / avg / poor. Good: two or three crisp segments with evidenced, distinct pains. Average: one plausible segment but pains asserted without grounding. Poor: no segmentation, pains are personal guesses. Common mistake: designing for the candidate's own use case and calling it the market.

Problem structuring

Does the candidate frame the problem with a clear structure — goal, then user, then pain, then solution — before committing to any answer?

✓ Strong looks like
  • States the goal and constraints up front, then narrows deliberately
  • Uses a visible framework so you can follow the reasoning step by step
✕ Watch for
  • Jumps straight to a feature in the first minute
  • Reasoning wanders with no organizing structure to return to

Suggested probes

  • Before we discuss solutions, how are you framing this problem?
  • What's the goal here, and what would you deliberately scope out?
  • Walk me through how you got from the goal to that area of focus.

Good / avg / poor. Good: explicit goal → user → pain → solution path you could redraw afterward. Average: some structure but skips a step or two. Poor: unstructured leap to features. Common mistake: solutioning before structuring — naming a feature before defining the user or the pain.

Product creativity

Does the candidate generate several differentiated, non-obvious ideas rather than stopping at the first obvious one?

✓ Strong looks like
  • Offers a spread of ideas that attack the pain from genuinely different angles
  • Includes at least one non-obvious idea and can explain why it's interesting
✕ Watch for
  • One idea, treated as the only possibility
  • "Ideas" that are near-duplicates of each other

Suggested probes

  • Give me three meaningfully different ways to solve this.
  • What's a solution most people wouldn't think of, and why might it win?
  • If that first idea were off the table, where would you go?

Good / avg / poor. Good: several distinct ideas including a non-obvious one. Average: two or three ideas that are variations on one theme. Poor: a single obvious idea. Common mistake: mistaking volume for range — listing many ideas that are all the same shape.

Prioritization

Does the candidate choose among ideas using explicit criteria — impact, effort, and strategic fit — rather than picking on instinct?

✓ Strong looks like
  • Names the criteria and weighs each idea against them out loud
  • Makes a clear call and owns the tradeoff it implies
✕ Watch for
  • Picks a favorite with no stated reason
  • Refuses to choose, hedging that "it depends" without resolving it

Suggested probes

  • Of those, which would you build first, and on what criteria?
  • How do impact, effort, and strategic fit trade off across them?
  • What would have to be true for your second choice to jump to first?

Good / avg / poor. Good: explicit, weighted criteria and a defended call. Average: a reasonable pick with thin justification. Poor: arbitrary choice, no criteria. Common mistake: listing criteria but never actually applying them to make the decision.

Success metrics

Does the candidate define success with a north-star metric plus guardrails tied to the stated goal, rather than vanity numbers?

✓ Strong looks like
  • Picks a north-star that captures real user value and ties to the goal
  • Adds guardrail metrics to catch the ways it could be gamed
✕ Watch for
  • Vanity metrics (raw signups, pageviews) with no link to value
  • One metric and no thought to what it might break

Suggested probes

  • How would you know this succeeded — what's the one metric you'd watch?
  • What guardrails would you set so that metric can't be gamed?
  • How does that metric ladder up to the goal we started with?

Good / avg / poor. Good: a goal-linked north-star with sensible guardrails. Average: a reasonable metric but no guardrails. Poor: vanity metrics disconnected from value. Common mistake: optimizing a single metric without naming what it could quietly harm.
