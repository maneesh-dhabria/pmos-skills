Technical round — interviewer reference

technical round · what to probe, what good and weak answers look like

Purpose of this round. This is a PM-flavored technical/system round, not a coding exercise. You are testing the technical judgment a product manager needs to partner credibly with engineering: can the candidate hold a mental model of a system, reason about tradeoffs, scope realistically, and speak the language of data and APIs — without pretending to be an engineer. Score the quality of reasoning, not whether they would write the code.

System understanding

Can the candidate decompose a product into its moving parts — clients, services, data stores, external dependencies — and trace how a request or a piece of data flows through them? You are looking for a coherent mental model, not encyclopedic component names.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Sketches components and the data flow between them unprompted
  • Names where state lives and what talks to what, at the right altitude
✕ Watch for
  • Treats the system as a black box; can't say what happens after a click
  • Lists buzzwords without showing how the pieces connect

Suggested probes

  • How would you design the notifications system end to end? Walk me from the trigger to the user's phone.
  • When a user submits this form, what happens between the click and the confirmation screen?
  • Where does the data for this feature live, and who reads or writes it?

Technical tradeoffs

Does the candidate recognize that engineering choices trade off against each other, and can they reason about the dimensions that matter to the product — latency, cost, complexity, consistency, reliability? You want them to weigh options, not just pick one.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Surfaces a tradeoff axis (e.g. real-time vs. cost) and reasons about both sides
  • Ties the technical choice back to a user or business consequence
✕ Watch for
  • Asserts one "right" answer with no awareness of the cost
  • Optimizes for an axis the product doesn't care about

Suggested probes

  • Should this feature update in real time or is a few minutes' delay fine? What does each choice cost us?
  • What would you give up to ship this twice as fast, and what would break if you did?
  • When is eventual consistency acceptable here, and when would it burn a user?

Engineering collaboration

Does the candidate know where the PM's input belongs and where the engineer's does? You want a partner who brings the problem, constraints, and priorities — and trusts engineering on the implementation — not someone who either rubber-stamps or dictates the solution.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Frames the problem and constraints, then asks eng for the how
  • Knows which decisions are theirs to make vs. the team's to make
✕ Watch for
  • Defers every technical question — "whatever eng thinks"
  • Over-specifies the implementation and crowds out the engineers

Suggested probes

  • An engineer pushes back that your approach is too expensive to build. How do you handle that conversation?
  • Where do you draw the line between what you decide and what the engineering team decides?
  • Tell me about a time you disagreed with engineering on a technical direction. What did you do?

Feasibility judgment

Can the candidate scope a realistic MVP and sequence the work by risk rather than by wishlist? You want to see them cut scope deliberately and tackle the scariest unknowns first.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Carves out a thin first slice and names what's deferred
  • Sequences by what's most likely to fail or surprise
✕ Watch for
  • Proposes a sweeping build with no phasing or risk ordering
  • Can't say what they'd cut if the timeline halved

Suggested probes

  • If you had four weeks instead of four months, what's the first thing you'd ship?
  • What's the riskiest assumption in this design, and how would you de-risk it early?
  • Which part of this would you build first, and why that part?

Data & API literacy

Is the candidate comfortable reasoning about data shapes, contracts between systems, and what to instrument? You're not asking them to write SQL — you want them fluent enough to spec an API, read a schema, and know what events they'd need to answer a product question.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Reasons about what fields/entities a feature needs and how they relate
  • Names the events or metrics they'd instrument to measure success
✕ Watch for
  • Can't describe the data a feature produces or consumes
  • Treats analytics and instrumentation as an afterthought

Suggested probes

  • What does the API for this feature need to return, and what would a consumer send it?
  • What events would you instrument to know whether this feature is working?
  • Walk me through the data model — what are the main entities and how do they relate?

Calibration

What a strong PM sounds like. They reason out loud, build a model with you, surface the tradeoff before you ask, and stay in their lane — bringing problem and priority, trusting eng on craft. They are comfortable saying "I'd ask an engineer to confirm, but my instinct is…" and then giving a defensible instinct.

Anchoring the scale. A 2 is someone who can follow the system once you draw it but never leads. A 3 builds the model and weighs one real tradeoff. A 4 does it unprompted, across multiple axes, and connects every technical choice back to the user.

Common mistakes (interviewer)

Don't reward the candidate who pretends to be an engineer. Confident jargon and a deep dive into algorithms is not the bar — and a PM who over-claims technical authority is a red flag, not a green one.

Don't penalize honest "I don't know." A PM who says "I'd defer that implementation detail to eng, but here's the constraint I'd give them" is showing exactly the judgment this round tests. Penalize abdication of reasoning, not abdication of coding.

Don't let one strong axis mask a weak one. A great system sketch with zero tradeoff awareness is a split score, not a uniform high one — score each dimension on its own.
