Behavioral — interviewer reference

behavioral round · what to probe, what good and weak answers look like

Purpose of this round. Test leadership and values through real past situations, not hypotheticals. Drive every answer to STAR — Situation, Task, the candidate's own Action, and the Result. Your job is to keep pulling the story toward what this person actually did, with measurable outcomes, until you can score from evidence.

Leadership & influence

Whether the candidate moves outcomes through people they don't manage — peers, partner teams, leadership — by building conviction rather than relying on a title or escalation.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Names specific people they persuaded and the argument that turned them
  • Outcome reached across a team boundary they had no authority over
✕ Watch for
  • "I told them to" — relied on authority or escalation as the first move
  • Influence claimed but no named person, decision, or change of mind

Suggested probes

  • Tell me about a time you got a team you didn't manage to change direction. How?
  • When did you have to lead without the formal authority to decide? What did you do?
  • Walk me through the hardest person you've had to bring around — what was your argument?

Conflict navigation

How the candidate handles real disagreement — with a peer, manager, or partner — whether they surface it directly and drive to a resolution that holds, versus avoiding it or kicking it up the chain.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Raised the disagreement directly with the person, early
  • Resolution that stuck and preserved the working relationship
✕ Watch for
  • Let it fester, went around the person, or escalated without trying first
  • "Won" the argument but the relationship or outcome was damaged

Suggested probes

  • Tell me about a time you disagreed with your eng lead — what happened?
  • Describe a conflict with a peer that you had to resolve yourself. How did it end?
  • When did a disagreement not go your way? What did you do next?

Ownership & bias for action

Whether the candidate takes end-to-end responsibility for outcomes and acts under ambiguity, versus waiting for direction, perfect information, or someone else to own it.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Stepped into an unowned problem and saw it through to the result
  • Acted on a reasonable call before the situation was fully clear
✕ Watch for
  • Blames process, manager, or other teams for a poor outcome
  • Waited to be told, or stalled until ambiguity resolved itself

Suggested probes

  • Tell me about a problem nobody owned that you picked up. What did you do?
  • Describe a time you had to act without clear direction. How did you decide?
  • Tell me about something that went wrong on your watch. What was your part in it?

Stakeholder management

How the candidate keeps execs, eng, and design aligned — proactively reading what each needs and getting ahead of misalignment, versus pushing information one direction and reacting once things break.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Tailored the message to what each stakeholder cared about
  • Surfaced and closed a misalignment before it became a problem
✕ Watch for
  • One-directional updates; treated stakeholders as a broadcast list
  • Only engaged a stakeholder after a conflict or escalation forced it

Suggested probes

  • Tell me about a time execs, eng, and design wanted different things. How did you align them?
  • Describe a stakeholder you struggled to keep on side. What did you change?
  • When did you catch a misalignment early? How did you spot it?

Self-awareness & growth

Whether the candidate reflects honestly on real failures and can name what specifically changed in how they work afterward — versus a polished non-answer or a "weakness" that's secretly a strength.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Names a concrete failure and their own contribution to it
  • Articulates a specific behavior change and how it played out later
✕ Watch for
  • "My weakness is I care too much" — humble-brag, no real reflection
  • Lesson stated in the abstract with no evidence it changed behavior

Suggested probes

  • Tell me about a time you failed. What did you do differently afterward?
  • What's a piece of feedback that was hard to hear? What changed because of it?
  • Looking back at that story — what would you do differently now?

Calibration

  • 4 — a real, owned STAR story per dimension with the candidate's own actions and a measurable result; you could retell it without them in the room.
  • 3 — a real situation with clear personal action, but the result is soft or the story needed pulling.
  • 2 — generic or team-credit answer; the candidate's specific contribution stays fuzzy even after probing.
  • 1 — hypothetical, deflective, or contradicts the role's bar; no real situation surfaces.

Common mistakes

  • Accepting a hypothetical. "I would…" is not evidence. Redirect: "Tell me about a specific time this actually happened."
  • Letting "we" stand. Vague team-credit hides whether the candidate did anything. Always ask "what did you personally do?"
  • Leading questions. Don't hand the candidate the answer ("So you escalated, right?"). Ask open questions and let them tell the story — leading is an interviewer anti-pattern that manufactures false signal.
  • Scoring on polish. A confident delivery of a thin story is still a thin story. Score the evidence, not the storytelling.
  • One story, one dimension. Don't let a single strong anecdote inflate every score — probe each dimension on its own.
