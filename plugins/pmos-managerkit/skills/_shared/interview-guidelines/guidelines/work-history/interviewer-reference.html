Work history — interviewer reference

work-history round · a structured chronological deep-dive · what to probe, what strong and weak answers look like

Purpose of this round. Reconstruct what the candidate has actually done — role by role, in order — and separate what they personally drove from what their team, their manager, or a good market did. The résumé is a claim; this round is the audit. It exists to calibrate level (is the scope of their real work at, below, or above the bar for this role?) and to catch the two failure modes a résumé can't: inflation (credit taken for others' work) and a partial arc (a title that outran the scope). It is not a behavioral round — the unit here is a role and its results, not a single STAR anecdote.
The method — a chronological funnel. Walk the career forward, oldest to most recent, and go deep on the roles that matter for this level. Within each role, peel the onion: start broad ("what was your charter?"), then narrow relentlessly to the specific decision, the specific action they took, and the measured result.
  1. Open (2 min) — frame the round: "I'd like to walk through your career in order, spending most time on the last couple of roles. I'll interrupt to go deep — that's a good sign, not a bad one."
  2. Career-arc scan (5 min) — a fast forward pass over every role: title, dates, company stage, and the one-line charter. Builds the skeleton and flags the roles to mine.
  3. Per-role deep-dive (25–30 min) — the core. For each significant role, run the funnel below until you can name the candidate's own contribution and its result.
  4. Level-targeted competency probes (throughout) — as evidence surfaces, score it against the Reforge/Mehta competencies weighted for this level (see level-ladder.md + scorecard.html). Probe the competencies that carry the most weight for the target level hardest.
  5. Wrap (5 min) — the boss-rating and reference probes, then the candidate's questions. Leave time; the manager questions often surface the truest signal.

1 · Career-arc scan

The fast forward pass. You are building the chronological skeleton and deciding where to spend your deep-dive budget — usually the two most recent roles and any role the candidate leans on.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Clean, honest timeline — dates, titles, and company stage line up with the résumé
  • Can state each role's charter in a sentence without inflating it
✕ Watch for
  • Vague or shifting dates; gaps or overlaps waved away ("around then")
  • Every role described as a promotion into ever-grander scope with nothing to anchor it

Suggested probes

  • Let's walk your career in order. Start with your first PM role — title, company, dates, and the one-line charter.
  • For each next role: what changed — the scope, the team, the mandate?
  • Which of these roles are you proudest of? We'll go deep there.

4 clean arc, honest framing, knows which roles to feature · 2–3 arc mostly clear but some scope inflation · 1 timeline doesn't reconcile with the résumé. Common mistake: letting the scan run long — it's a skeleton, not the interview.

2 · Per-role deep-dive (the funnel)

The heart of the round. For each significant role, peel the onion: charter → the specific problem → the decision → their action → the result. Do not accept the first, broad answer; every "we" and every abstraction is a place to narrow.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Can go three or four levels deep without running out of specifics
  • Names the hard trade-off, the option not taken, and why
  • Detail is consistent under repeated narrowing — the story holds
✕ Watch for
  • Runs out of detail two questions in — the story was a headline, not a memory
  • Answers get vaguer, not sharper, as you narrow (the inflation tell)
  • Jargon and frameworks substituted for what actually happened

Suggested probes (the peel)

  • What was the single hardest decision you owned in that role? Walk me to the moment you decided.
  • What were the options? Why did you kill the one you killed?
  • Who disagreed, and what did you do about it?
  • If I called your eng lead from that project, what would they say you specifically did?
  • What did you get wrong, and when did you find out?

4 depth holds under four levels of narrowing, specifics stay consistent · 2–3 good headline, thins out under pressure · 1 cannot get past the abstraction. Common mistake: accepting a polished narrative and moving on — the polish is exactly what you must drill through.

3 · Contribution attribution — "we" vs "I"

The most important discipline in the round. A résumé is written in "we"; your job is to find the "I". Every time the candidate says "we", you want to know what they did inside that we — not to diminish teamwork, but to size their personal contribution at level.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Distinguishes their work from the team's unprompted — "the team shipped X; my part was Y"
  • Generous with credit and precise about their own lever
  • Owns the parts that were theirs, including the failures
✕ Watch for
  • "We" the whole way through; can't or won't isolate their own contribution
  • Claims the team's big win but distances from its failures ("that was eng")
  • Individual-contribution stories dressed up as leadership, or vice-versa

Suggested probes

  • You keep saying "we" — what was your specific part of that?
  • What would not have happened if you hadn't been there?
  • Where did your contribution end and someone else's begin?

4 crisp, honest self/team boundary at the right altitude for level · 2–3 isolates contribution when pushed · 1 "we" all the way down. Common mistake: reading confident "we" language as leadership — press for the "I".

4 · Results & measurement — the inflation catch

Did it work, and how do they know? Strong PMs quantify and can also tell you what the number doesn't capture. This is where résumé inflation surfaces: an outcome that shrinks under questioning, a metric with no baseline, credit for a tailwind the market provided.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Names the metric, the baseline, and the delta — and its limits
  • Separates their impact from the market/tailwind honestly
  • Comfortable saying "we didn't measure that well" when true
✕ Watch for
  • Big percentages with no baseline, no denominator, no timeframe
  • The result shrinks each time you ask for specifics (the classic inflation curve)
  • Company-level outcomes claimed as personal impact

Suggested probes

  • You said it grew 40% — 40% of what, from what baseline, over what window?
  • How much of that was you versus the market or the rest of the company?
  • What's the number you're least proud of from that role?

4 quantified, baselined, honest about attribution and limits · 2–3 real results but fuzzy on baseline/attribution · 1 numbers evaporate under scrutiny. Common mistake: being satisfied by a big number — always ask "of what, from what, over what window".

5 · Level & trajectory

Synthesise the arc. Is the scope of their real work at, below, or above the bar for this role? Read scope (feature → area → product → portfolio → org) and ownership language against the level-ladder.md scope anchors. The partial-arc catch lives here: a title one rung above the evidence.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Scope of real work matches (or exceeds) the target level's anchor
  • A clear growth story — each role a genuine step up in ambiguity or scope
  • Self-aware about where they are on the ladder and why
✕ Watch for
  • Title outran the work — a "Senior/Lead" whose every example is a single feature
  • Flat arc — same scope for years, relabeled
  • Lateral hops that read as escaping before results landed

Suggested probes

  • What was the biggest jump in scope between two of your roles? What made it a jump?
  • At your last level, how many PMs / what portfolio did you actually own?
  • What's the largest ambiguous problem you've owned end-to-end?

4 scope at/above bar, clear upward arc · 2–3 at-bar but arc is uneven · 1 title above evidence (partial arc). Common mistake: scoring the title, not the scope — the ladder reads the work.

6 · Management read & reference verification

The wrap. Two Topgrading staples: the boss-rating probe (a low-cost honesty test) and a soft-pedalled look at how references would land. Both are read for self-awareness, not as gotchas.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Predicts a manager's rating honestly, incl. the critique they'd give
  • Comfortable naming past managers and what they'd say — no defensiveness
  • Owns a development area without either false modesty or spin
✕ Watch for
  • Every past boss rated a 9–10 with no critique — implausible, low self-awareness
  • Evasive or hostile about references (a flag worth its own follow-up)
  • "My only weakness is I work too hard"

Suggested probes

  • On a 1–10 scale, how would your last manager rate your performance? Why that number, not one higher?
  • What would they say you needed to work on?
  • When we take references, who should we talk to — and what will they tell us that we haven't covered?

4 honest self-rating with a real critique, open on references · 2–3 mostly candid, some polish · 1 uniformly high ratings, no self-critique. Common mistake: skipping the "why not one higher?" follow-up — it's where the boss-rating probe earns its keep.

Bias register — check yourself before you score. The work-history round is unusually exposed to interviewer bias because it runs on narrative. Before writing the scorecard, re-read your notes against these:
BiasHow it shows up hereCounter
HaloOne impressive role or logo colours every dimensionScore each role's evidence on its own; a great Series-A story doesn't earn the strategy dim
ConfirmationYou decided in the first five minutes and mined for supportActively hunt for the disconfirming detail; run the peel even when you like them
LeadingYou feed the candidate the answer inside the questionAsk open, then narrow with facts — never with the conclusion you want

Vagueness toleranceYou accept a smooth abstraction as an answerTreat every unfilled specific as an open question, not a pass
