Case study — interviewer reference

case-study round · what to probe, what strong and weak work looks like

Purpose of this round. A take-home / case study: the candidate is given a written prompt and works it asynchronously over a fixed window (typically a few days), submitting a deliverable — a memo, deck, or model. You evaluate the submitted work first, on its own merits, then optionally run a short live debrief where the candidate walks their thinking and you probe the gaps. This round tells you how a PM reasons when they have time, no interviewer to react to, and have to make the case in writing to a busy stakeholder. Score the work and the debrief; do not penalize for not reading your mind on scope the prompt left open — reward candidates who name that ambiguity and choose a path.

Additional doc: the case prompt

The actual case prompt / take-home brief is not in this file. It is role-specific and lives in this round's guidelines/case-study/additional/ folder, registered in the role's role.json under additional_docs[]. That brief is what the candidate received — its stated question, given data, constraints, and deliverable format are the ground truth for what a "good answer" even is.

When grounding scores against the prompt (e.g. "the brief asked for a 6-month plan but the candidate scoped 18 months"), cite the brief as evidence at data-cite-tier="notes" — it is supplied context, not a transcript line the candidate spoke. Read it before scoring so you judge the candidate against the question actually asked, not a different one you imagined.

Evaluation areas

Problem framing

How the candidate interprets the brief before solving it — whether they take the stated question literally or step back to find the decision that actually matters, and whether they surface the assumptions they had to make to proceed. The prompt almost always leaves scope deliberately open; framing is where you see judgment about what to solve.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Restates the real underlying problem, not just the prompt's surface ask
  • Names the assumptions and open questions, then picks a defensible scope
✕ Watch for
  • Solves the literal prompt without questioning whether it's the right question
  • Hidden assumptions that, if wrong, invalidate the whole analysis

Suggested probes

  • What did you decide this case was really asking, and why that over the literal prompt?
  • Which assumptions were load-bearing — and what would change if one were wrong?
  • What did you deliberately leave out of scope, and how did you decide?

Analytical depth

The rigor under the conclusions — whether claims are reasoned from evidence (the data in the brief, sensible estimates, a structured breakdown) or asserted. Look for sizing, sensitivity to the inputs, and trade-offs that are actually weighed rather than listed.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Quantifies the key drivers and shows the math / estimation logic
  • Pressure-tests their own answer — sensitivity, edge cases, second-order effects
✕ Watch for
  • Confident conclusions with no supporting analysis underneath
  • Ignores data that was sitting in the brief, or misreads it

Suggested probes

  • Walk me through the numbers behind your headline recommendation.
  • Which input is your conclusion most sensitive to — and how confident are you in it?
  • What did you do with the data in the brief that you didn't end up showing?

Structure & clarity

Whether the deliverable lands for its reader — a busy exec who skims. Strong work leads with the answer, sequences the argument so each step earns the next, and is navigable without the author present. This is about the shape of the communication, not its polish (that's craft).

✓ Strong looks like
  • Answer-first; the recommendation and its logic are clear in the first minute
  • A reader can follow the argument top-to-bottom with no backtracking
✕ Watch for
  • Buries the conclusion; reader must reconstruct the point from the body
  • Disorganized — sections don't build, ideas repeat or contradict

Suggested probes

  • If you had one slide / one paragraph for the CEO, what's on it?
  • How did you decide the order to present this in?

Recommendation quality

Whether the candidate actually makes a call and stands behind it. Strong recommendations are specific (you could act on them Monday), defensible (the reasoning holds under push), and honest about their own risks and the next steps to de-risk. The failure mode is a tour of options with no decision.

✓ Strong looks like
  • One clear, specific recommendation with the reasoning that forced it
  • Names the top risks and a concrete next step / what would change their mind
✕ Watch for
  • Hedges — lists options without choosing, or "it depends" with no resolution
  • A bold call with no acknowledgement of what could go wrong

Suggested probes

  • What's the single biggest risk to your recommendation, and how would you de-risk it?
  • What evidence would make you reverse this call?
  • What are the first two things you'd do in week one to act on it?

Communication & craft

The finish on the deliverable — concision, clean visuals, no errors. With days to work, a sloppy submission (typos, broken charts, padding) is itself signal about care and standards. Weighted lightest, but a careless deliverable undercuts trust in everything above it.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Concise and well-supported; visuals carry their weight and are legible
  • Polished — no typos, broken links, or filler; respects the reader's time
✕ Watch for
  • Careless errors, broken visuals, or obvious padding despite ample time
  • Volume over substance — long, but says little

Suggested probes

  • What would you cut if you had to halve the length?
  • How long did this take, and where did the time go?

Running the round

Calibration

  • Judge against the brief, not your ideal. Read the case prompt in additional/ first; score the question actually asked.
  • The deliverable is the primary artifact. Form your score from the submission before the debrief; use the debrief to confirm or revise, not to rescue weak work.
  • Open scope is a feature, not a trap. Reward candidates who name the ambiguity and choose; don't dock them for a reasonable path you wouldn't have taken.
  • Weight depth and the call. Analytical depth and recommendation quality carry the round (25 each); a tidy deck with no real analysis or no decision is a low score.
  • Anchor on the 1→4 descriptors on the scorecard before settling a number.

Common interviewer mistakes

  • Scoring against a different question than the brief posed.
  • Rewarding polish over substance — a beautiful deck that decides nothing.
  • Penalizing a defensible scope choice because you'd have scoped it differently.
  • Letting a strong live debrief paper over a thin written deliverable (or vice versa).
  • Halo from one dazzling section bleeding into every dimension's score.
