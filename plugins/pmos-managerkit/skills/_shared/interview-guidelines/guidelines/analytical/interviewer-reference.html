{{round_name}} — interviewer reference

analytical round · what to probe, what good and weak answers look like

Purpose of this round. This round tests the candidate's rigor with metrics, execution, and diagnosis: can they define the right measure, decompose a fuzzy problem cleanly, form and prioritize hypotheses to isolate a driver, size things from defensible assumptions, and weigh tradeoffs against the metric rather than personal preference. You are scoring how they think under quantitative pressure, not whether they reach your preferred answer.

Metric definition

Whether the candidate picks a precise, decision-relevant metric and can say why it beats the obvious alternatives.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Names a specific metric, ties it to the user/business goal, and notes a guardrail metric
  • Rejects a tempting vanity metric and explains the rationale
✕ Watch for
  • Reaches for "engagement" or "DAU" with no definition of who counts or over what window
  • Optimizes a single number with no awareness of what it could break

Suggested probes

  • You said you'd track engagement — define it precisely. What's the numerator and denominator?
  • If that metric went up but the business got worse, what would have happened?
  • What guardrail metric would you watch alongside it?

Problem structuring

Whether the candidate breaks an ambiguous question into a clean, mutually-exclusive set of parts before diving in.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Lays out a MECE decomposition (e.g. by funnel stage, segment, or supply vs demand) before analyzing
  • States assumptions and scopes the question explicitly up front
✕ Watch for
  • Jumps to a favorite cause with no framework
  • Buckets that overlap or leave obvious gaps

Suggested probes

  • Before you solve it — how would you break this problem into parts?
  • Are those buckets exhaustive? What case falls outside them?
  • Which branch would you go down first, and why that one?

Hypothesis & diagnosis

Whether the candidate generates prioritized hypotheses and uses data to isolate the actual driver rather than guessing.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Ranks hypotheses by likelihood × ease-of-checking and says what data would confirm each
  • Slices the metric (segment, platform, cohort, time) to narrow to the cause
✕ Watch for
  • Fixates on one cause and never considers competing explanations
  • Proposes data pulls with no theory of what they'd rule in or out

Suggested probes

  • DAU dropped 8% week-over-week — how do you find out why?
  • You have three hypotheses — which do you test first and what data settles it?
  • The dashboards look normal but the number's still down. What now?

Estimation & sizing

Whether the candidate sizes a quantity from transparent, sanity-checked assumptions rather than a pulled-from-air number.

✓ Strong looks like
  • States each assumption out loud and builds the estimate top-down or bottom-up cleanly
  • Sanity-checks the result against a known anchor and flags the biggest source of error
✕ Watch for
  • Produces a confident number with no visible assumptions
  • Arithmetic so tangled the logic can't be followed or checked

Suggested probes

  • How many of feature X would we serve per day? Walk me through it.
  • Which assumption is your answer most sensitive to?
  • Does that number pass a sanity check against something you already know?

Tradeoff reasoning

Whether the candidate weighs real options against the chosen metric and commits to a defensible call instead of a one-sided pitch.

✓ Strong looks like
  • Compares at least two options on cost, impact, and risk against the metric
  • Makes a clear recommendation and names what would change their mind
✕ Watch for
  • Argues only the upside of their preferred option
  • Lists pros and cons but never lands a decision

Suggested probes

  • Ship the simple fix now or the robust one in two sprints — which, and why?
  • What does each option cost us on the metric you defined earlier?
  • What evidence would flip your recommendation?

Calibration & common mistakes

What each level looks like

Good (3–4)
Defines the metric before touching numbers, decomposes cleanly, prioritizes hypotheses and uses slices to isolate the driver, sizes from stated assumptions, and lands a defensible call weighed against the metric.
Average (2–3)
Gets to a reasonable answer but needs prompting for structure, leaves a hypothesis or two unchecked, or makes a decision without fully pricing the alternative.
Poor (1–2)
Dives into arithmetic before defining the metric, guesses at causes without a framework, or argues one side and never commits.

Common mistakes

  • Diving into the numbers before defining the metric being optimized
  • Treating a single hypothesis as the answer without ruling out competitors
  • Quoting an estimate with no visible, checkable assumptions
  • Listing tradeoffs but refusing to make a recommendation
