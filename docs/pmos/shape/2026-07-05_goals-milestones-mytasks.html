Goals & milestones in /mytasks

Shaped problem · 2026-07-05 · via /shape

The shaped problem (TL;DR)

Maneesh's goals live outside /mytasks — in his head, a doc, or a new-year note — so day-to-day they go invisible and he steers by his reactive task list instead of by his goals. And even when he does recall a goal, he has no trustworthy signal of whether he is on schedule against its checkpoints or starving it of attention — so he cannot tell whether his daily work is adding up to what he set out to do.

Who: Maneesh, sole user of his personal /mytasks system. When: during daily / weekly task triage (goals are absent from the surface he actually works on) and in recurring moments of doubt — "am I doing enough?" — where no signal exists to answer honestly. Why now: /mytasks has matured (projects, subtasks, due dates, people, recurrence); goals are the missing top layer everything else could hang from.

This is a problem brief. It deliberately proposes no solution — the goals → milestones → attachment model you sketched is captured below as a proposed direction for /requirements, not as a shaped decision. See Handoff.

Felt problem (HMW + JTBD)

HMW: How might we make Maneesh's goals a durable, ever-present part of /mytasks so they stop vanishing — and give each goal an honest read of whether he's on pace, both on schedule and in attention?

JTBD: When Maneesh plans his day / week, or wonders whether he's actually making progress, he wants his goals present in the tool he already works in and a trustworthy per-goal pace signal, so he can steer his work by his goals instead of only reacting to his task list — and catch a goal that's drifting before it's too late.

Felt pain (confirmed this session): the root is capture — goals were never in the system (they live in his head / a doc), so they're invisible where he works. On top of that, "doing enough" is genuinely two signals, both wanted: schedule adherence (hitting dated checkpoints) and attention allocation (not letting a goal starve). A goal can be on-schedule but starved, or well-fed but slipping — he wants to catch either.

Competing framings (ripple on the framing)

Framing A — "Goals as a reporting layer." Goals collect attached tasks and roll up a count / % complete. Ripple: gives you a number, but the number is a weak proxy for "enough"; it leans hard on diligent attachment (stale-data trap) and doesn't inherently put the goal in front of you. Rejected as the core — it's a rollup, not a rudder.

Framing B — "Goals as a steering layer." ✅ CHOSEN (unified framing). Goals are present in the daily flow and carry a dual pace signal (schedule + attention) you actually steer by. Ripple: forces surfacing and a trustworthy signal to be first-class, not by-products of a rollup; more to build, and it must solve signal-trust (below) or it collapses back into Framing A's noise.

Framing C — "Goals as a periodic reckoning." A weekly / monthly ritual puts goal-vs-actual in your face. Ripple: strong for accountability, but you de-prioritized it against "goals always present." Kept as a possible surface for Job B (e.g. a /morning-brief or weekly-review channel), not the frame.

Chosen framing: B — a steering layer, carrying both pace signals.

Ceiling-breaker (mandatory off-deck probe)

Ceiling-breaker: Off-deck probe — the signal can recreate the very problem it solves. This is not a generic lens; it's the dimension most likely to sink the build. A pace-sensing system has two self-defeating failure modes. (1) Crying wolf on open-ended goals — "stay healthy", "keep learning" have no finish line, so a schedule-adherence signal flags them "behind" forever and you learn to ignore it. (2) Stale attachment data — the "starved / neglected" read is only as true as your discipline in attaching tasks; if attaching is friction, the data rots and the tool confidently tells you you're neglecting a goal you've actually been working. Either way the reckoning becomes noise, you tune it out, and goals go invisible again — through avoidance instead of absence. Design implication: any solution has to earn trust in its signal (goal types with/without deadlines; frictionless or automatic attachment; a way to say "this is a maintenance goal, don't alarm me"), not merely display a signal.

Open questions (for /requirements)

  • Quantifying "enough" (attention signal): what defines fed vs. starved — a recency threshold ("no task in N days"), a count, time logged, or a per-goal target cadence?
  • Open-ended goals: do habitual/maintenance goals get a mode with no schedule-behind alarm? How is a goal typed as dated vs. open-ended?
  • Cascade semantics: if a project is attached to a goal, is inheritance to its tasks hard (all tasks, always) or overridable per task? What happens when a task moves projects?
  • Milestone dates vs. existing model: how do milestone dates relate to task due-dates and recurrence? Can a milestone be "met" without every attached task being done?
  • Progress source of truth: is goal/milestone progress derived from attached-task completion, or a manual value the user sets — or both?
  • Surfacing channel: where do goals appear so they stop vanishing — a new /mytasks view, the daily agenda, and/or /morning-brief?
