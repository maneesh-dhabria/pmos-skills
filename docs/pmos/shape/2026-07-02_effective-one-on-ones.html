Effective 1:1s — a skill for pmos-managerkit

Shaped problem · 2026-07-02 · via /shape

The shaped problem (TL;DR)

A manager lacks a durable, low-friction, per-report thread that (a) lets them quick-capture a discussion item the instant it occurs, (b) assembles a tailored, human-first agenda at the moment each 1:1 starts, and (c) carries continuity across sessions — open action items, recurring themes, and career goals — so weekly 1:1s stop decaying into status updates and growth / morale actually get tended.

Who: any manager with recurring direct reports. When: the minutes before each 1:1 (prep), the moments after (capture), and any moment mid-week when a discussion item occurs and needs to be jotted in two seconds so it surfaces next session.

The skill is opinionated / actively coaching (user decision): it bakes in cross-source 1:1 best practices — intent-tagged question suggestions, human-check-in-first, and nudges when recent sessions read status-heavy or an action item has gone stale — rather than being a neutral note organizer. There is no solution design here by charter; that begins downstream at /skill-sdlc define.

Felt problem (HMW + JTBD)

HMW: How might we make it effortless for a manager to walk into every 1:1 prepared and human-first, and never lose the thread between sessions?

JTBD: When a recurring 1:1 with a report is imminent (or just ended), a manager wants the relevant context, the right questions, and last time's open loops in one place, so they can run a conversation that grows the person instead of re-running a status readout.

Felt pain (confirmed working assumption A1): a pairing of blank-page / no-prep + no-memory / no-continuity, with status-update creep as the anti-pattern the skill actively fights and self-mirror / coaching as an emergent benefit.

Competing framings (ripple on the framing)

Framing A — "Prep assistant." Skill = generate an agenda before each 1:1. Ripple: light and fast, but forgets everything → continuity lives elsewhere → status-creep returns. Rejected as the core.

Framing B — "Per-report relationship thread." ✅ CHOSEN. Skill = a durable, grounded per-report record that prep, continuity, action items, and career goals all hang off of, refreshed each session. Ripple: higher value; guards status-creep structurally by always surfacing growth / human sections; natural fit with /people; interops with /interview-feedback's person model. More to build.

Framing C — "Coaching mirror." Skill = grade / improve the manager. Ripple: niche, hard to ground, weak fit with managerkit's artifact pattern → de-scoped to a sub-feature, not the core.

Resolved: the continuity unit

One rolling record per report — internally a reverse-chronological log of dated session entries plus persistent top sections that never scroll away. (Resolves the user's open question "one recurring record vs. one per session.")

  • Persistent header (rarely changes): who they are, cadence, career goals / growth focus, standing themes, current open action items, operating-manual answers (how they like feedback / recognition).
  • Running agenda / inbox (mid-week quick-capture): un-discussed discussion items dumped in two seconds, cleared into the next session entry when raised. This is the surface the "quickly dump notes" job writes to.
  • Session log (append each 1:1, newest first): date, human check-in, topics, decisions, action items (owner + status), questions surfaced.

Rationale: one-file-per-session fragments continuity (the exact pain being killed); a flat rolling blob becomes unread. The hybrid is what GitLab's public 1:1 handbook, Lara Hogan's per-person doc, and the "shared rolling doc" doctrine all converge on.

Research grounding — 1:1 best practices

Sourced synthesis (primary sources fetched where possible) to feed the skill's baked-in coaching. Full brief retained in session; condensed here.

Durable cross-source principles (the coaching floor)

  1. It's the report's meeting — agenda & tone set by them (Grove, Horowitz, GitLab, Scott, Manager Tools).
  2. Not a status report — move status async; use the time for the human (Rands, Fournier, Scott, Zhuo).
  3. Listen far more than you talk — Horowitz 10/90, GitLab 20/80.
  4. Show up consistently; near-never cancel — the slot itself is the signal (Grove, Manager Tools, Fournier "oil changes").
  5. High-leverage, not overhead — Grove: 90 minutes shapes two weeks of output.
  6. Go below the surface / embrace the awkward (Zhuo; Rands' Vent / Disaster modes).
  7. Solicit feedback on yourself, then stay silent — Scott's "What could I do or stop doing…" + "count to six".
  8. Capture notes & close the loop on action items — Grove's "handshake"; GitLab Hallway tags.
  9. Coaching & growth are the point — Google Oxygen behavior #1; run periodic career conversations separately (Laraway).
  10. Learn how each person wants to be led — early — Hogan's first-1:1 operating-manual questions.
  11. Match style to the person & moment — Fournier's five 1:1 styles; Grove's task-relevant maturity.
  12. Protect the human layer deliberately — especially remote (GitLab).

Named models to encode

  • Manager Tools 10/10/10 — report's agenda / manager's agenda / the future.
  • Rands — Update · Vent · Disaster — diagnose which mode you're in; on a Vent, don't problem-solve.
  • Laraway Career Conversations — Life Story → Dreams → Career Action Plan, run as dedicated sessions.
  • GitLab agenda template — Performance / Career / Vision / Hallway, with revisit-timing tags.
  • Hogan first-1:1 operating-manual questions — feedback style, recognition preference, "what makes you grumpy."

Question bank by intent

  • Opener: "What's top of mind for you right now?" (Zhuo)
  • Growth / career: "What skills would you like to develop?"; Laraway's Life Story / Dreams prompts.
  • Blockers: "What can I do to get out of your way?"; "What's the #1 problem with our org? Why?" (Horowitz)
  • Morale: "Are you happy here?"; "When have you felt demoralized this year?"
  • Feedback-up: "What could I do or stop doing that would make it easier to work with me?" (Scott)
  • Relationship (first 1:1): "How do you like feedback — medium and timing?"; "How do you prefer recognition?" (Hogan)

Attribution caveats to honor downstream

  • The 10/90 talk ratio is Horowitz, not Grove (Grove's version: "ask another question").
  • Laraway's plan is "long-term vision + short-term plan" (Sandberg's 18-month), not a "15-month plan".
  • Hogan's "Bias Toward [Action/Feelings]" two-column exercise is unverified (likely her book Resilient Management) — verify before quoting.
  • Manager Tools 10/10/10 and the Rands "you're doing it wrong" line are documented-canon / paraphrase, not fetched verbatim this run.
