Abandoned side projects

Created using pmos‑toolkit

The shaped problem

A solo maker loses the felt sense of progress on a side project during the multi-day gaps between sessions, and by the time they sit back down the project feels like dead weight rather than momentum — so they quietly abandon it within about three weeks, while still wanting to be the kind of person who finishes things.

Who / when / why-now: a hobbyist building alone in evenings/weekends, at the moment of re-opening the project after a 3–7 day gap; why-now is personal — a pattern of four abandoned repos this year.

This is a problem brief. It deliberately proposes no solution — see Handoff below.

Felt problem (HMW + JTBD)

How might we: help a solo maker re-enter a paused side project still feeling the momentum they had when they last closed it?

Who: a hobbyist maker working alone, no external accountability.

When / where: the re-entry moment after a multi-day gap, at the desk, deciding whether to open the project or something else.

Job-to-be-done: "When I come back to a project after a break, I want to immediately feel where I was and that it was going somewhere, so I can keep being someone who ships."

Falsifiable hypothesis: If we solve the re-entry momentum gap, then the share of projects surviving past week three observably rises.

Abstraction ladder

Climbing why / descending how to find the right altitude (5-Whys run because "I abandon projects" read as a symptom).

  • Symptom: "I abandon my side projects."
  • Why → coming back after a gap feels like starting cold.
  • Why → nothing reflects the prior momentum back to me at re-entry.
  • Why → progress lived only in my head, not in anything I re-encounter.
  • Too high: "I lack discipline" — true but not actionable and not the leverage point.

Chosen altitude: the re-entry moment — concrete enough to act on, general enough to cover all four abandoned projects.

Decomposition

Sub-problems, with the real one marked ★.

  1. Forgetting what I was doing (state recall).
  2. ★ Losing the felt momentum — the emotional sense it was going somewhere (the real one; recall alone doesn't re-motivate).
  3. Guilt accumulation making re-opening aversive.
  4. Competing novelty — a newer idea is shinier at the re-entry decision.
