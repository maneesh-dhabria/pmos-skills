Honest fit

For product people who work in the terminal.

✓ This is for you if…

  • you're a PM, founder, or builder who already works in Claude Code or Codex;
  • you want to ship end-to-end and learn the craft as you go;
  • you want AI output you can trust — cited sources, a skeptic on every plan;
  • you'd rather compose small, sharp skills than learn one monolith.

✕ Not for you if…

  • you want a hosted SaaS dashboard — this lives in your terminal;
  • you need a no-code, point-and-click tool;
  • you expect an autonomous agent that ships unsupervised — you are the gate at every step;
  • you don't use Claude Code or Codex.

A skeptic is built into every step

Most AI tooling agrees with you. These passes argue back — surfacing the problems before they reach code. Here's what /grill actually does:

🧑‍💼 pmos-managerkit · do manager work

Do the manager work — grounded in evidence, not memory.

Who it's for: the hiring manager or lead interviewer who needs a defensible record after an interview round. The first skill is /interview-feedback; team reviews, 1:1 prep, and calibration land here over time.

Two grounded artifacts from one round of inputs

Drop in a recording (transcribed locally), interviewer notes, and the role's scorecard. You get back:

a filled scorecard
Every dimension scored on its own scale, flags ticked, an overall hire / no-hire — with every subjective claim grounded in a ≥40-character verbatim quote from the transcript. A deterministic gate refuses any output where a citation isn't actually in the transcript.
interviewer coaching notes
Per interviewer, scored against a researched effectiveness rubric — questioning structure, probing depth, leading-question avoidance, bias mitigation.
/interview-feedbacksetupscore
The scorecard /interview-feedback fills — weighted dimensions, anchored scales, green/red flags. Nothing scored without a quote to back it.

🛠️ pmos-utilities · keep it all healthy

The supporting layer.

Who it's for: anyone running the kit. These aren't part of a feature pipeline or a learning artifact — they're the meta-tools you reach for to keep your machine, and the skills themselves, in good shape.

/mac-health
Diagnose a hot, slow, or battery-hungry Mac — orphaned processes, extension leaks, sleep blockers — read-only first, then recommends. You decide.
/reflect
A paste-back retro of every pmos skill you used this session, severity-tagged, ready to hand to the skill author.
/converter
A zero-dependency local converter — JSON↔YAML, CSV↔JSON — running entirely in your browser against a local server.
/to-notion-doc
Parse a local .md/.html/.txt into a Notion page — never drops a table column, verifies block-for-block after write.
Why it's different

Honest by design.

Most AI tooling over-promises and quietly hallucinates. pmos-skills builds the skeptic into the workflow — so the output survives contact with reality. There are no testimonials on this page, because none have been collected yet; we'd rather show you the rigor than borrow credibility we don't have.

adversarial passes

It argues back.

/grill interviews you on shaky assumptions; /msf-req and /msf-wf simulate user friction; /simulate-spec pressure-tests the design before a line of code.

verified-source

It never invents a citation.

Learnkit fetches every link and claim this run — if it wasn't fetched, it doesn't ship. The interview scorecard refuses any rating without a verbatim quote behind it.

offline · self-contained

It runs without the network.

Games, primers, frameworks, specs — and this very page — are self-contained HTML that open from file://. No CDN, no account, no telemetry.
