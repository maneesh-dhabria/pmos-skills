## Hero / first fold (first 5 seconds)

Okay, clean. White, lots of air, a little purple "p" logo, nav says Plugins / Ship / Learn / Manage. My eye goes straight to the big black headline: **"Ship features, learn any topic, and run the manager work — from your terminal."** My honest first reaction: that's... three different promises in one sentence. "Ship features, learn any topic, AND run the manager work" — my brain does a tiny stall. Is this a dev tool? A learning thing? A manager thing? It's trying to be all of them, which makes me slightly suspicious it's a grab-bag rather than one sharp thing.

The eyebrow above it — "A MARKETPLACE OF CLAUDE CODE & CODEX PLUGINS" — actually helps more than the headline. *Oh, it's a plugin marketplace.* That's the real "what is this." I kind of wish that clarity were IN the headline.

The terminal mockup on the right is the thing that actually sells me in the first 5 seconds. I can read it: `/feature-sdlc "add CSV export..."` then a cascade — /requirements, /grill, /spec, /plan, /execute, /verify, /complete-dev → `shipped.` That's concrete. That tells me what using this actually feels like more than the headline does. Nice. The little checkmarks and "4 shaky assumptions surfaced" are a good touch — feels like a real run, not lorem ipsum.

Subhead: "pmos-skills is the product manager's toolkit for Claude Code & Codex: **60 skills across 5 plugins**. Each one produces a real, openable artifact — and the delivery pipeline pressure-tests itself with a built-in skeptic at every step."

NOW I get it — "product manager's toolkit." That's the who. I wish that word "product manager" were higher up; it's the single most useful disambiguator and it's buried in body copy. As a PM, *that's* my "oh this is for me" moment, and it came at second 8, not second 2.

"60 skills across 5 plugins" — slightly intimidated. 60 is a lot. Is that a flex or a warning that I'll have to learn 60 commands? Mixed feeling.

"runs offline / 0 network calls" in the stat row — I like that, genuinely. That's a trust signal for a busy skeptic.

Two CTAs: "Add the marketplace" (purple) and "See the 5 plugins". Single dominant CTA, good. Though "Add the marketplace" assumes I already know what that means and that I'm ready to commit — I'm not yet. I'd probably click "See the 5 plugins" first.

Verdict on hero: I understand it (eventually), but the headline made me work for it. I would NOT bounce yet — the terminal demo bought you another 20 seconds. Scrolling.

## Final verdict

**Emotional one-liner:** I came in skeptical of yet another over-promising AI toolkit and left mildly impressed by how relentlessly *honest* it is — the "skeptic built in" + "no testimonials, here's the rigor instead" framing disarmed me more than any feature did.

**Would I install it? — MAYBE (leaning yes).** Yes because: it's clearly for someone like me (PM in the terminal), it's low-commitment (install only what you need, runs offline, zero-setup smoke test), and the anti-hallucination mechanisms are specific and falsifiable, not just adjectives. Not a clean yes because: the headline tries to be three things at once and made me work to "get it," 60 skills feels like a lot of surface area (padded by games I'll never use), and it's visibly a solo/indie project with no external proof yet — I'd click "View on GitHub" and try one skill before betting my workflow on it. So: I'd install ONE plugin (toolkit) and kick the tires, not "add the marketplace" wholesale.

**The 3 moments that most shaped that feeling:**
1. The **/grill terminal demo** ("Why not in the gateway, where a transient 503 never even reaches the user?") — the single most credible proof on the page; turned an abstract claim into something I believed.
2. The **"Not for you if…" column** and the **"no testimonials… we'd rather show you the rigor than borrow credibility we don't have"** copy — these two honesty moves did more for my trust than any feature list could.
3. The **headline stall** in the first 5 seconds ("ship features, learn any topic, AND run the manager work") + the **games section** — the two moments my skeptic flared, making me feel it's a broad grab-bag rather than one sharp tool.
