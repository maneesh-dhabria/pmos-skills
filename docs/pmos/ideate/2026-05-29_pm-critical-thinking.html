PM Critical-Thinking Dojo

Created using pmos‑toolkit

TL;DR

A manually-triggered pmos-learnkit skill that runs a time-banded practice session in which a PM works through a varied mix of critical-thinking exercise shapes — each scoped to a real product-management problem domain, each carrying its own generation + evaluation spec — reasoning in their own words wherever possible, with feedback that grades the reasoning moves rather than the answer.

Recommendation: Go. Strong, well-scoped idea. The three differentiating bets are (1) grading reasoning-moves not answer-correctness, (2) a varied exercise-shape engine, (3) time-banded sessions sized to the user's available minutes. The two biggest risks — LLM grading reliability and scenario believability — are both cheaply testable before any real build.

How Might We

How might we give product managers a low-friction, repeatable way to practice and measurably improve critical thinking — sized to the minutes they have, scoped to problems they actually face, and forcing them to reason in their own words — without it feeling like homework?

Target user & job-to-be-done

Who: Product managers and product builders (IC to senior), and anyone who makes product judgment calls under ambiguity.

When / where: A spare 5–30 minutes — between meetings, commuting, or warming up before a real decision. Triggered manually inside a terminal/CLI session (often within a working repo).

JTBD: "When I've got a few spare minutes — or I'm staring at a hard product decision — help me sharpen my reasoning under ambiguity and show me I'm getting better, without it feeling like a test."

Hypothesis

We believe that a time-banded session of mixed, PM-scoped reasoning drills graded on critical-thinking moves (not answer correctness) will produce repeat voluntary practice and a rising, visible reasoning-quality / calibration score over sessions because PMs improve judgment through deliberate, varied reps with concrete feedback far faster than through passive reading, and a measurable signal sustains the habit.

Idea variants considered

Generated via First Principles + Analogous Inspiration. Critical thinking decomposed into ~8 named PM muscles: surface assumptions · steelman alternatives · trace 2nd-order effects · evidence-vs-inference · falsifiability ("what would change your mind") · spot bias · reframe the question · causal reasoning. ★ = selected for the v1 exercise library (9 shapes, plus the mix-engine delivery).

  1. ★ Scenario → pick & defend — realistic PM dilemma + 3–4 defensible options; grade the reasoning, not the pick. (Seed.)
  2. ★ Hard mode / open-response — no options; free-form decision + rationale, graded against a moves rubric.
  3. ★ Assumption-hunt drill — show a confident memo; list the load-bearing assumptions + cheapest test for each.
  4. Steelman-then-break — argue for a decision, then against it; kills first-idea anchoring. (Deferred to v2.)
  5. Socratic interrogation — user states a position; skill asks one probing question at a time (practice-mode /grill). (Deferred to v2.)
  6. ★ Calibration / forecasting — predict a probability + reasoning; track a Brier score over reps for objective improvement.
  7. ★ Spot-the-bias gym — short reasoning snippet seeded with a fallacy (sunk cost, survivorship, confirmation); name the flaw.
  8. ★ "What would change your mind?" — given a strong opinion, state the disconfirming evidence; trains falsifiability.
  9. ★ Reframe-the-question — stakeholder says "build X"; reframe to the underlying problem before answering.
  10. ★ Second-order consequence map — propose a change; trace 2nd/3rd-order effects; compare to a consequence tree.
  11. Red-team a PRD — feed a flawed spec; find the reasoning gaps. Doubles as real-work practice. (Deferred to v2.)
  12. ★ Daily-rep / time-banded session (delivery engine) — variable mix of the above, sized to the minutes the user picks.
  13. ★ Bring-your-own-dilemma — paste a real decision you're stuck on; run the moves rubric live.
  14. Decision-journal + retro loop — log a real call now with a prediction; structured retro later. Highest transfer.
  15. Compare-to-expert trace — after answering, show how a pro reasoned. Learning by contrast (needs a corpus; v2).

How it works (chosen finalist)

  • Surface: a Claude Code slash-command skill in pmos-learnkit, triggered manually (e.g. /dojo or /critical-thinking). Not app-like; persistence is a lightweight local scorecard, not a server.
  • Commitment band (first prompt, AskUserQuestion): Quick 5–10 min → 2–3 exercises · Standard 15–20 min → 4–5 · Deep ~30 min → 6–8 · Marathon 45 min+ → keep going until the user stops. Bands are proposed; user picks.
  • Mix engine: each session draws a varied set of exercise shapes from the v1 library (below); each shape is a spec with two halves — how to generate the scenario and how to evaluate / probe the answer. Rotation avoids repeating the same shape and weights toward under-practiced muscles.
  • v1 exercise library (9 shapes, user-selected):
    • Core loop — Pick & defend (MC via AskUserQuestion + own-words defense) · Hard mode (free-form open response) · Bring-your-own-dilemma (run the rubric on a real decision).
    • Targeted single-muscle drills — Assumption-hunt · Spot-the-bias gym · "What would change your mind?" (falsifiability).
    • Analysis & measurement — Calibration / forecasting (accumulating Brier score = the objective progress signal) · Second-order consequence map · Reframe-the-question.
    Deferred to v2 (need persistence/corpus, not selected): decision-journal + retro loop, compare-to-expert trace, Socratic interrogation, steelman-then-break, red-team-a-PRD.
  • Problem scope: PM / product-builder domains only — product design, prioritization & tradeoffs, metrics & experimentation, influence & stakeholder management, strategy under ambiguity, GTM. Not "everything under the sun."
  • Reason in your own words: free-form answers are the default, with explicit instructions on the expected answer shape/length. AskUserQuestion is used only for genuinely multiple-choice shapes (pick-and-defend, spot-the-bias); when that tool is unavailable, fall back to numbered free-form prompts with clear instructions.
  • Repo-aware (don't force it): when run inside a repo, 1–2 exercises are generated from repo context if a clean product-decision framing is derivable; otherwise stay generic. Every session keeps at least 1–2 generic (non-repo) problems regardless.
  • Grading: feedback evaluates the reasoning moves (assumption surfaced? alternative steelmanned? 2nd-order effect traced? falsifiable claim stated?) and names what was missing — never just "great answer."
  • Progress signal: a per-session scorecard persisted locally (e.g. ~/.pmos/learnkit/) tracks weakest muscle and accumulating calibration (Brier) over sessions — the visible "you're getting better" proof.

Premortem: failure modes

It is 2027-05-29. This skill shipped 12 months ago and has failed. Why?

ModeLikelihoodMitigation
The AI's grading is mushy praise — every answer gets "great reasoning!", so users learn nothing and churn after a few sessions.HGrade against a fixed per-shape moves rubric; require at least one concrete "you did NOT do X" critique per answer; never grade on the choice.
Generated scenarios are generic "SaaS dashboard" pablum that no real PM finds believable, so engagement dies.HCurate a seed bank of PM problem archetypes per domain; generation riffs on archetypes (not a blank page); calibrate with worked examples in the spec.
Stateless slash-command means no longitudinal "you improved" signal — the core motivation (measurable progress) is invisible.HPersist a per-session scorecard to ~/.pmos/learnkit/; accumulate calibration + weakest-muscle trend across sessions.
Free-form-everywhere makes a session feel like an exam; friction spikes and users bounce after one try.MTime-band sizing + interleave fast MC/spot-the-bias reps; cap each free-form answer at ~3 sentences; lead with low-stakes warm-ups.
Repo-context generation misfires — pulls irrelevant code, feels forced, or surfaces sensitive detail.MStrict "only if a clean product-decision framing is derivable" gate; cap repo exercises at 1–2; never quote secrets; always keep generic problems too.

Success signals

Leading: session completion rate; share of answers given free-form vs. multiple-choice; repeat sessions within a week; actual exercises completed vs. the chosen band.

Lagging: calibration (Brier) score improving across sessions; per-muscle scores rising, especially the weakest; self-reported transfer ("I used a move from the dojo in a real decision").
